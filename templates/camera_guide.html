🚧 Narrow Net Setup (~30° angle) — Most Users

Most nets are 2.5–3m wide — too narrow for a pure side-on camera. The solution is a ~30° angled setup from the bowler's end.

Setup
  • Position: Behind bowler's crease, ~2m to off side
  • Distance: 15–18m (full pitch length)
  • Height: Batter's chest height
  • Zoom: 2× optical (no digital zoom)
  • Orientation: Landscape (horizontal)
Why this works
  • Ball trajectory is visible the full length
  • Bat swing path and face angle readable
  • Head position relative to stumps is clear
  • Foot placement relative to the crease is visible
  • Works in any net width >2m

🎯 Recommended: Side-on, Off Side (90°)

This is the industry standard for batting analysis. It gives you the clearest picture of the batter's technique.

Why the off side?
  • You see the bat face opening toward the camera — critical for judging swing path
  • Front elbow and shoulder position are clearly visible
  • Spine angle and side-on body shape are readable
  • The ball's line relative to off stump is visible
  • Head position over the front knee is evaluable
🎥 Ideal Two-Camera Setup

If you have two phones/cameras, this is the gold standard:

Camera 1: Side-on (Off side)

For technique analysis — shape, swing, balance. Place 10 m from popping crease at chest height.

Camera 2: Front-on (Bowler's end)

For line & length analysis — ball tracking, foot placement, bat face angle.

📱 Recording Tips (Phone)
  • 📌 Use a tripod — steady footage is essential
  • 🌞 Good lighting — avoid backlight / silhouettes
  • 👤 Full body visible — head to toe in frame
  • 📐 Landscape mode (horizontal) always
  • ⚡ 1080p at 30fps or 60fps is ideal
  • 🎯 Avoid digital zoom — move closer instead
  • 🔇 Record with sound — bat-on-ball impact helps phase detection
  • 📏 Mark 22 yards (pitch length) in frame for speed calibration
Pro tip: For the most accurate ball speed and trajectory, include both sets of stumps in the frame and ensure the calibration markers (22-yard pitch) are visible.
